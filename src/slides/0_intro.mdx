Notes: I want to talk about something much larger than computers being hard

I want to make the argument that many seemingly-distinct problems have a shared cause:
the near-absence of basic digital infrastructure for science.

Rather than being trivial or unavoidable, I will argue that digital deinfrastructuring
- defines the everyday reality of science, with harms personal
  and systemic, present and future.
- can be solved! but an ethical, empowering solution is not inevitable -- indeed
  we are on track to create an immensely more terrifying triple-dip industry
  strapped to the back of public funding for science
- and **decentralization** is the best means of liberatory, globally empowering
  digital infrastructure.

To do that
- I'll take some lessons from other knowledge communities: specifically pirates,
  the rad hippie wing of internet architects, librarians, STS.
- And sketch basic vision of what decentralized digital infrastructure for science
  could look like, how we get there, and how we are trying to get started at ION

# 1) What are the symptoms of digital deinfrastructuring?

# As a Labor System:

- Fundamentally altered the practice of science so that almost every single field
  is half programming now
- Scientific **labor is prodigiously reduplicated**. reinventing
  everything and redoing the same experiments in every lab, and the ethical
  catastrophe for animal research that entails
- Most of that labor falls on **ECRs**, creating a labor system where lack of infrastructure
  is papered over by always-expanding needs for cheap labor, armies of undergrads
  clicking through videos, cleaning data, grad students thrown to the wolves of amateur engineering
- The production of an underclass of technical laborers that do a large proportion
  of the *work* of science but don't have access to the traditional reward mechanisms
  of science (ie. papers)
- Technical and resource knowledge silos make the *rich get richer* as wealthy labs
  and institutes can afford to hire engineers and build sophisticated core facilities,
  do a qualitatively different kind of science, and maintain increasingly disproportionate
  funding

# As a System of Scientific Inquiry:

- Holds science back from its naive characterization as a cumulative, self-correcting
  system.
- Profoundly leaky knowledge system where entire PhDs worth of data are lost and
  rendered useless as a matter of regular practice just by forgetting what format it's in
  or where it's stored.
- Inevitability of replication crises because it's impossible to replicate experiments
  on rigs that were built once and never documented
- Stunted scientific discourse on for-profit platforms that don't come close to
  satisfying our needs. The scientific consensus exists as a disorderly pile of hundreds of
  thousands of isolated pdfs who only reference each other via the exhausting labor
  of exhaustive brute force literature searches. That, or godforsaken science twitter
- The potential futility of understanding the brain from the limited viewpoint
  of data from a single or small number of labs, or the bureaucratically limited viewpoint
  of a few large institutes like Google Brain.

# Science and its relationship with society

- An absconscion with the public resources we are stewards of, and rather than ensuring that
  every time we solve some technical problem, or learn something new we make it available
  to the public in a medium they can access. Instead we largely make paywalled pdfs,
  in part contributing to public distrust of science --- why would they trust us when
  they have no idea what we do?

The most profound impact of scientific deinfrastructuring on society will come from
what will happen if we do nothing...
